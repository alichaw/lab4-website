About Me

Background &
Motivations

I'm a student based in Taipei, Taiwan, with a strong focus on systems programming and cybersecurity. My academic journey has been driven by a fascination with how computers work at their most fundamental level—from hardware abstractions up through operating system design and into the application layer.

My primary technical interests revolve around low-level systems: I write in C and Rust, study the Linux kernel internals, and enjoy understanding how memory, concurrency, and resource management interact beneath the surface. Rust in particular excites me because it challenges programmers to reason rigorously about ownership and safety without sacrificing performance.

On the security side, I actively participate in Pwnable and Capture The Flag (CTF) challenges. These competitions push me to understand how programs break—through stack overflows, heap exploitation, format string vulnerabilities, and reverse engineering—which in turn makes me a more thoughtful and defensive software engineer.

What I find most compelling is the tension between these two disciplines. High-performance systems demand speed and minimal overhead, while robust security requires careful validation, isolation, and often additional layers of defense. I'm actively learning how to strike that balance—building systems that are both fast and provably safe—and I believe the research community's advances in memory-safe systems languages and formal verification are pointing toward exciting answers.

Beyond the technical work, I enjoy reading systems papers, contributing to open-source projects, and collaborating with peers who share a curiosity for the way things work under the hood.
